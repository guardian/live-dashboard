What is the SEO Dashboard?

The SEO Dashboard started as a hack day project towards the end of 2011. It aimed to provide fast feedback when our content is promoted on external sites such as Google News, by showing recent page popularity and referrers.

After the hack day completed, the SEO team pleaded with us not to switch it off, and so that dashboard was kept running on a developer machine for their use on an ad-hoc basis. Over time, it became a home for exploring the feasability and value of building tools around site usage.

How do you collect the data?

The initial version of the SEO dashboard took advantage of the fact that (nearly) all requests for pages on the Guardian desktop website come to our servers for processing. We processed 10% of the web server logs that this generated and used this to calculate popularity.

Although broadly successful, there are a number of limitations with this approach:

  • it's difficult to distinguish between human traffic and automated (bot) traffic
  • it's difficult to determine unique browsers
  • it's impossible to identify what link users clicked to navigate between pages

Earlier this year, we switched to collecting data in the same way as other analytics providers such as Site Catalyst and Google Analytics do, by adding a small piece of JavaScript to all pages on www.guardian.co.uk. This code pings back information about navigation to servers running in the Amazon Web Services cloud. We call this our clickstream.

The full historical clickstream is stored for potential offline analysis, using big data processing tools such as Apache Hadoop or Amazon's Elastic Map Reduce. At the time of writing, we haven't pursued this very much.

The last week's full clickstream is stored online (using elasticsearch) for interactive querying. The traffic related tools in this dashboard are driven off that online clickstream.

What about our sites other than www.guardian.co.uk?

The tracking is only present on www.guardian.co.uk. At the moment there's no intention to expand the prototype.

Is this a fully-supported offical product?

No. The SEO Dashboard and associated tools are a prototype, designed to explore the cost, feasibility and value of us processing our own clickstream. Development and maintenance are done on a best effort basis, so although all of the people involved are passionately excited about the potential of this approach, no one is working on it full time.

If you're finding any of the tools useful, or have suggestions or ideas, please drop us a line and let us know.

What’s ophan?

When the SEO dashboard moved from developers desktop to the internet, we needed a domain name. As a prototype, it wasn't appropriate to place on a guardian domain, so we had to find something else. In true startup style, a combination of wikipedia and searches for available (cheap) domain names, ended up at ophan, the "many-eyed ones":

The ophanim or ofanim, also Ophde (Hebrew "wheels" אְוּפַּנים; singular ofan) refer to the wheels seen on Ezekiel's vision of the chariot (Hebrew merkavah) in Ezekiel 1:15-21. These are first construed as angels in one of the Dead Sea scrolls (4Q405), and as a class of celestial beings in late sections of the Book of Enoch (61:10, 71:7) where with the Cherubim and Seraphim they never sleep, but guard the throne of God.

These "wheels" have been associated with Daniel 7:9 (mentioned as gagal, traditionally "the wheels of gagallin", in "fiery flame" and "burning fire") of the four, eye-covered wheels (each composed of two nested wheels), that move next to the winged Cherubim, beneath the throne of God. The four wheels move with the Cherubim because the spirit of the Cherubim is in them. These are also referred to as the "many-eyed ones" in the late Second Book of Enoch (20:1, 21:1).
